## Step 1: Define Your Receptionist’s Role and Scope

Don’t just “get an AI receptionist.” Decide exactly what you want it to do. Start with one core job, then expand.

**Common AI receptionist use cases:**

* **Inbound call answering:** “Thanks for calling [Business Name]. How can I help you?”
* **Appointment scheduling:** “Would you like to book a consultation?”
* **FAQ answering:** “Our hours are 9–5 Monday through Friday.”
* **Lead qualification:** “Are you looking for a quote or just gathering information?”
* **After‑hours message taking:** “We’re closed now, but I can take your details.”
* **Call routing:** “Press 1 for sales, 2 for support, 3 for billing.”

**Worksheet: Define your AI’s responsibilities**

1. What are the **top 5 questions** callers ask? (e.g., “What are your hours?”, “Do you accept insurance?”, “How much does X cost?”)
2. What **actions** should callers be able to take? (book, get a quote, speak to a person, leave a message)
3. What **information** does the AI need to answer those questions? (price lists, service menus, holiday closures)
4. When should the AI **transfer to a human**? (complex complaints, existing‑customer issues, emergencies)

Example: A real‑estate agency might want their AI to answer basic questions about listings, schedule property tours, and qualify buyer vs. seller calls—then transfer to the right agent.

## Step 3: Design the Conversation Flow (Script That Converts)

This is the heart of your AI receptionist. A good script feels natural and gets the job done. A bad script feels robotic and frustrates callers.

**The 4‑part call structure:**

1. **Greeting** (5–10 seconds)  
   Friendly, brand‑aligned opening. Example: “Hi, thanks for calling [Business Name]. This is [AI Name]. How can I help you today?”

2. **Identification & routing** (15–30 seconds)  
   Ask a few key questions to understand the caller’s intent. Use open‑ended questions: “Are you calling to schedule an appointment, get a quote, or speak with someone specific?”

3. **Task execution** (variable)  
   Handle the caller’s request: book an appointment, answer a FAQ, take a message, qualify a lead.

4. **Closure** (5–10 seconds)  
   Confirm next steps, thank the caller, and end the call politely. “I’ve booked you for Tuesday at 2 PM. You’ll get a text reminder. Thanks for calling!”

**Pro tip:** Write your script like a human conversation—not a flowchart. Use contractions (“I’m”, “you’ll”), occasional pleasantries (“Sure, I can help with that”), and avoid jargon.

## Step 4: Configure Your Phone Number and Business Rules

Your AI needs a phone number to answer. You have three options:

1. **Get a new number from the platform** (easiest). VoxReach lets you pick a local or toll‑free number in seconds.
2. **Port your existing business number** (takes 1–2 weeks, but keeps your brand identity).
3. **Forward calls from your current number** (quick stopgap, but can add latency).

**Essential business‑rule settings:**

* **Business hours:** When the AI answers live vs. takes messages.
* **Holiday closures:** Dates the office is closed.
* **Call‑routing rules:** “During business hours, route sales calls to extension 101, support to 102.”
* **Voicemail fallback:** What happens if the AI fails? (e.g., “I’m having trouble hearing you. Let me transfer you to a human.”)

**Important:** Test your number before going live. Call from a different phone, try different scenarios, and listen to the audio quality.

## Step 5: Train Your AI with Your Business Knowledge

Even the smartest AI needs to know about your business. Feed it:

* **FAQ document:** A simple list of questions and answers.
* **Service/product catalog:** Descriptions, prices, durations.
* **Team directory:** Who does what, extensions, availability.
* **Policies:** Cancellation fees, payment terms, insurance accepted.

Platforms like VoxReach let you upload a PDF or paste text—the AI automatically extracts the relevant knowledge and uses it during calls.

**Training best practices:**

* Start with **50–100 Q&A pairs**. You don’t need to anticipate every question, just the common ones.
* Use **clear, concise language**. Avoid long paragraphs.
* Include **alternative phrasings**. “What are your hours?” / “When do you open?” / “Are you open on Sundays?”
* **Review call logs weekly** to spot questions the AI missed, then add them to the knowledge base.

## Step 6: Go Live and Monitor Performance

Don’t “set and forget.” Launch in stages:

1. **Soft launch (week 1):** Route 20% of your calls to the AI. Keep your human receptionist on the line to catch failures.
2. **Ramp‑up (weeks 2–3):** Increase to 50%, then 80% as confidence grows.
3. **Full automation (week 4+):** 100% of calls handled by AI, with human fallback for escalations.

**Key metrics to watch:**

* **Answer rate:** What percentage of calls does the AI answer? (Target: >95%)
* **Call duration:** Average talk time. (Should be similar to human calls.)
* **Completion rate:** How many calls achieve their goal? (booking, qualification, etc.)
* **Sentiment score:** Are callers happy, neutral, or frustrated?
* **Escalation rate:** How often does the AI transfer to a human? (Ideal: 10–20%—too low means the AI may be missing complex issues; too high means it’s not handling enough.)

## Common Pitfalls (And How to Avoid Them)

* **The robot voice:** Choose a platform with high‑quality voices. Test multiple voice options—some sound warmer, some more authoritative.
* **Over‑engineering the script:** Keep it simple. Start with 5–7 conversation nodes, not 50.
* **Ignoring compliance:** If you’re doing outbound calls, you must scrub against Do‑Not‑Call lists. Use a platform that builds compliance in.
* **Not telling callers they’re talking to AI:** Be transparent. “This is an AI assistant” in the greeting avoids deception and sets expectations.
* **Skipping the soft launch:** Go slow. A bad first impression can turn customers off for good.
